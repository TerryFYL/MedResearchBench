## Abstract Summary
This cross-sectional study with longitudinal mortality follow-up analyzed participants aged 20-79 years from two national surveys: NHANES III (1988-1994) and NHANES 2007-2012. Obstructive lung disease was defined using pre-bronchodilator spirometry (FEV1/FVC < LLN based on Hankinson 1999 reference equations). Participants were classified as diagnosed (self-reported physician diagnosis of COPD, emphysema, or chronic bronchitis) or undiagnosed. Among those with spirometric obstruction, 71.2% in NHANES III and 72.0% in NHANES 2007-2012 were undiagnosed. In multivariate models, undiagnosed status was consistently associated with self-reported good/excellent health, lower comorbidity burden, higher lung function, and racial/ethnic minority status. Among NHANES III participants (median follow-up 14.5 years), both undiagnosed (HR: 1.23; 95% CI: 1.08-1.40) and diagnosed participants (HR: 1.74; 95% CI: 1.45-2.09) had higher all-cause mortality risk than those without obstruction. These findings demonstrate that undiagnosed obstructive lung disease is common, has remained unchanged over two decades, and carries increased mortality risk despite the appearance of better health in cross-sectional assessment.

## Key Methods
- Cross-sectional analysis of two national surveys (NHANES III, NHANES 2007-2012)
- Spirometric obstruction defined by FEV1/FVC < LLN (Hankinson 1999 reference equations)
- Diagnostic status classified by self-reported physician diagnosis
- Survey-weighted prevalence estimation with stratification
- Survey-weighted multivariable logistic regression for factors associated with undiagnosed status
- Cox proportional hazards models for mortality analysis (NHANES III linked to National Death Index)
- Complex survey design accounted for (MEC weights, PSU, strata)
- NHANES 2007-2012 combined with 6-year weights

## Key Findings
1. Among those with spirometric obstruction: 71.2% undiagnosed in NHANES III, 72.0% in NHANES 2007-2012 — unchanged over two decades
2. Factors associated with being undiagnosed: good/excellent self-rated health (OR: 2.24), lower comorbidity burden, higher FEV1% predicted, racial/ethnic minority status (NH Black OR: 1.78, Mexican American OR: 2.43)
3. Factors associated with being diagnosed: more respiratory symptoms, more severe obstruction (GOLD III/IV), greater healthcare utilization, current/former smoking
4. Undiagnosed participants had increased all-cause mortality (HR: 1.23; 95% CI: 1.08-1.40) compared to those without obstruction, after 14.5 years median follow-up
5. Diagnosed participants had even higher mortality risk (HR: 1.74; 95% CI: 1.45-2.09)
6. Undiagnosed participants appeared healthier cross-sectionally (better self-rated health, fewer comorbidities, better lung function) yet still faced excess mortality

## Dataset Used
NHANES III (1988-1994) for longitudinal mortality analysis; NHANES 2007-2012 (3 combined cycles) for contemporary prevalence estimation
