## Abstract Summary
This study examined temporal trends in the association between sociodemographic and geographic factors and colorectal cancer staging and survival among Medicare beneficiaries aged 65+ using SEER-Medicare data from 1991 to 2010. A total of 327,504 CRC cases and 102,421 CRC deaths were analyzed across four time periods reflecting changes in Medicare CRC screening reimbursement: Period 1 (1991-1997, pre-screening coverage), Period 2 (1998-June 2001, FOBT+sigmoidoscopy), Period 3 (July 2001-2005, colonoscopy added), Period 4 (2006-2010, highest screening uptake). Multinomial logistic regression assessed factors associated with CRC stage, and Cox proportional hazards models assessed survival. Black patients were 24-39% more likely to present with distant disease compared to White patients. Residence in high-income areas was associated with a 7-12% reduction in distant-stage disease. Despite increasing screening availability, racial and socioeconomic disparities in CRC staging and survival persisted across all four time periods.

## Key Methods
- Retrospective cohort study using SEER-Medicare linked data (1991-2010)
- Multinomial logistic regression for stage at diagnosis (localized/regional/distant vs. unstaged)
- Cox proportional hazards models for CRC-specific mortality
- Four time periods reflecting Medicare screening policy changes
- Adjusted for age, sex, tumor site, comorbidity (Charlson Index), treatment, area-level income, and rurality
- N = 327,504 CRC cases; 102,421 CRC deaths

## Key Findings
1. Black patients 24-39% more likely to present with distant-stage CRC than White patients across all periods
2. High-income areas associated with 7-12% reduction in distant-stage diagnosis
3. Asian patients had 32% lower CRC mortality (1991-1997), but this advantage narrowed to 13% by 2006-2010
4. Black patients had 16-21% increased CRC mortality compared to White patients
5. Sociodemographic disparities in staging and survival persisted despite expanded screening coverage
6. Rural residence was not consistently associated with later-stage diagnosis after adjusting for income

## Dataset Used
SEER-Medicare Linked Database (1991-2010)
