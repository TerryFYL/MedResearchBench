# Related Work: Sleep Duration and Depression

## Reference 1
- **Title**: How Long Should We Sleep? Exploring the Relationship Between Sleep Duration and the Prevalence of Depression
- **Authors**: Hansen Li, Guodong Zhang, Yang Cao, Haodong Tian, Haowei Liu, Mingyue Yin, Xing Zhang
- **Journal**: International Journal of Behavioral Medicine
- **Year**: 2025
- **DOI**: 10.1007/s12529-025-10427-1
- **PMID**: 41419688
- **Relevance**: Uses NHANES 2017-2020 data with restricted cubic spline (RCS) analysis to demonstrate U-shaped sleep-depression relationship. Identifies optimal weekday sleep of 7.5-7.8 hours and weekend sleep of 8.0-8.7 hours. Complements Dong et al. by using the gold-standard nonlinear method (RCS) and a more recent NHANES cycle.

## Reference 2
- **Title**: Association between sleep duration and depression in menopausal women: a population-based study
- **Authors**: Feng Zhang, Long Cheng
- **Journal**: Frontiers in Endocrinology
- **Year**: 2024
- **DOI**: 10.3389/fendo.2024.1301775
- **PMID**: 38440789
- **Relevance**: NHANES 2011-2020 study in menopausal women (n=3,897) confirming U-shaped sleep-depression association with inflection at 7.5 hours using piecewise linear regression. Demonstrates the sleep-depression U-shape generalizes to vulnerable subpopulations with hormonal influences.

## Reference 3
- **Title**: U-shaped association between average daily sleep duration and depression among individuals with hypertension: a cross-sectional study based on NHANES 2017-2020
- **Authors**: Zhimao Cai, Ye Ye, Sixia Chen, et al.
- **Journal**: BMC Psychiatry
- **Year**: 2025
- **DOI**: 10.1186/s12888-025-07075-w
- **Relevance**: NHANES 2017-2020 study in hypertensive patients (n=2,912) showing U-shaped relationship with inflection at 7.29 hours. Provides evidence that the nonlinear sleep-depression pattern persists in a comorbid population where both sleep disturbance and depression are prevalent.

## Reference 4
- **Title**: Sleep Duration and Depression Among Adults: A Meta-Analysis of Prospective Studies
- **Authors**: Zhai L, Zhang H, Zhang D
- **Journal**: Depression and Anxiety
- **Year**: 2015
- **DOI**: 10.1002/da.22386
- **PMID**: 26010487
- **Relevance**: Meta-analysis of prospective cohort studies demonstrating that both short sleep (RR = 1.31) and long sleep (RR = 1.42) predict incident depression. Provides longitudinal evidence supporting the U-shaped cross-sectional findings from NHANES studies, addressing temporality concerns.
