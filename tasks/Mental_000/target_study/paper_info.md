## Abstract Summary
This cross-sectional study examined the association between sleep duration and depression among 25,962 U.S. adults using NHANES 2009-2016 data. Depression was measured by PHQ-9 (score >= 10). Sleep duration was categorized and analyzed with multivariable logistic regression and piecewise linear regression. After adjusting for demographic, socioeconomic, and lifestyle covariates, a U-shaped association was identified with an inflection point at 8 hours. Compared to 8 hours of sleep, both short sleep (OR = 1.86, 95% CI: 1.59-2.17) and long sleep (OR = 1.49, 95% CI: 1.22-1.83) were significantly associated with higher depression odds. Below 8 hours, each additional hour of sleep was associated with 32% lower odds of depression (OR = 0.68, 95% CI: 0.64-0.73); above 8 hours, each additional hour was associated with 32% higher odds (OR = 1.32, 95% CI: 1.18-1.48).

## Key Methods
- Multivariable logistic regression with progressive covariate adjustment
- Piecewise linear regression with inflection point estimation
- Sleep duration analyzed as both categorical (reference: 8h) and continuous variable
- Covariates: age, sex, race/ethnicity, education, marital status, income-to-poverty ratio, BMI, smoking, alcohol, physical activity, chronic conditions
- Complex survey design with NHANES MEC weights, PSU, strata
- Pooled 4 cycles (2009-2016) with weight adjustment

## Key Findings
1. U-shaped association: both short and long sleep duration significantly associated with depression
2. Inflection point at 8 hours of sleep per night
3. Short sleep (<8h): OR = 0.68 per additional hour (protective with increasing sleep)
4. Long sleep (>8h): OR = 1.32 per additional hour (risk with increasing sleep)
5. Categorical analysis: <6h (OR = 2.73), 6h (OR = 1.75), 7h (OR = 1.22) vs. 8h reference; 9h (OR = 1.28), >=10h (OR = 2.35)
6. Strongest associations at the extremes: <6h and >=10h sleep

## Dataset Used
NHANES 2009-2010, 2011-2012, 2013-2014, 2015-2016 (4 cycles pooled)
