# Target Study: Blood Heavy Metals and Cognitive Function in U.S. Older Adults

## Metadata
- **Title**: Association between serum iron, blood lead, cadmium, mercury, selenium, manganese and low cognitive performance in old adults from National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES): a cross-sectional study

- **Journal**: British Journal of Nutrition
- **Year**: 2023
- **Volume/Issue**: 130(10):1743-1753
- **DOI**: 10.1017/S0007114523000740
- **PMID**: 36941743

## Abstract Summary
This cross-sectional analysis used pooled NHANES 2011-2014 data (n=2,002 adults aged 60+ years) to examine associations between blood/serum concentrations of six trace elements (serum iron, blood lead, cadmium, mercury, selenium, and manganese) and low cognitive performance. Cognitive function was assessed using three standardized neuropsychological tests: CERAD Word List Learning and Recall (memory), Animal Fluency Test (verbal fluency/executive function), and Digit Symbol Substitution Test (DSST, processing speed/sustained attention). Low cognitive performance was defined as scoring below the 25th percentile on each test. Survey-weighted univariable and multivariable logistic regression analyses were used to assess associations. After adjusting for covariates, elevated serum iron levels were associated with decreased risk of low cognitive performance, especially in elderly without diabetes history and with hypertension history. High blood cadmium levels were associated with increased odds of low cognitive performance in older adults with diabetes and hypertension history. Blood lead was associated with increased odds of low cognitive performance. Selenium showed a protective association against cognitive decline.

## Key Methods
- Two pooled NHANES cycles (2011-2012, 2013-2014) with 4-year combined weights
- Blood metals measured by ICP-MS (CDC Division of Laboratory Sciences): Pb, Cd, Hg, Se, Mn
- Serum iron measured as part of standard biochemistry panel
- Cognitive assessment: CERAD (immediate/delayed recall), Animal Fluency Test, DSST
- Low cognitive performance: score below 25th percentile
- Survey-weighted multivariable logistic regression
- Covariates: age, sex, race/ethnicity, education, poverty income ratio, BMI, smoking, alcohol, diabetes, hypertension
- Subgroup analyses stratified by diabetes and hypertension history
- Complex survey design accounted for (NHANES weights, PSU, strata)

## Key Findings
1. Elevated serum iron associated with decreased risk of low cognitive performance across multiple cognitive domains
2. Blood cadmium associated with increased odds of low cognitive performance, especially in those with diabetes and hypertension comorbidity
3. Blood lead associated with increased odds of low cognitive performance
4. Blood selenium showed protective association against cognitive decline
5. Blood manganese connected with low cognitive performance
6. Effect modification by diabetes and hypertension status — metals' associations with cognition differed by comorbidity burden
7. Multiple cognitive domains affected: memory (CERAD), executive function (AFT), and processing speed (DSST)

## Dataset Used
NHANES 2011-2014 (2 combined cycles): cognitive function testing + blood metals panel in adults 60+ years
