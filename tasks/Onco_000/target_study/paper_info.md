## Abstract Summary
This study estimated and compared temporal trends in 1-year and 5-year CRC cause-specific survival in the United States by stage at diagnosis and race/ethnicity using SEER 12 registries data from 1992 to 2018. A retrospective cohort of 399,220 individuals diagnosed with CRC was analyzed. Significant differences in stage-specific survival trends were found across five racial/ethnic groups (non-Hispanic White, non-Hispanic Black, non-Hispanic American Indian/Alaska Native, Hispanic, non-Hispanic Asian/Pacific Islander). Differences were most pronounced for distant-stage CRC, where survival probabilities increased most consistently for AIAN and NHB persons, though their trend lines remained lower than those of Hispanic and other groups. Despite overall improvements in adjusted CRC survival since 1992, AIAN and NHB persons continue to experience worse prognosis than other racial/ethnic groups.

## Key Methods
- Retrospective cohort study using SEER 12 registries (1992-2018)
- Kaplan-Meier estimation of 1-year and 5-year CRC cause-specific survival
- Time trend analysis by stage (localized, regional, distant) and race/ethnicity
- Five racial/ethnic groups: NHW, NHB, AIAN, Hispanic, API
- N = 399,220 CRC cases

## Key Findings
1. Overall CRC survival improved from 1992 to 2018 across all stages and racial/ethnic groups
2. Stage-specific 1-year survival trends showed significant differences by race/ethnicity
3. Distant-stage CRC: survival improvements were most consistent for AIAN and NHB, but absolute survival remained lower than for Hispanic and other groups
4. AIAN and NHB persons continued to experience worse prognosis despite narrowing gaps
5. Localized-stage CRC showed the least racial/ethnic variation in survival trends
6. Disparities were more pronounced in 5-year survival than 1-year survival

## Dataset Used
SEER 12 Registries (1992-2018): Alaska, Connecticut, Atlanta, Rural Georgia, San Francisco-Oakland, San Jose-Monterey, Hawaii, Iowa, Los Angeles, New Mexico, Seattle-Puget Sound, Utah
