# Target Study: Depression Prevalence Trends in the United States

## Metadata
- **Title**: Trends in depression among Adults in the United States, NHANES 2005-2016
- **Authors**: Binbin Yu, Xueyuan Zhang, Chunpeng Wang, Mengzi Sun, Lina Jin, Xin Liu
- **Journal**: Journal of Affective Disorders
- **Year**: 2020
- **Volume/Pages**: 263: 609-620
- **DOI**: 10.1016/j.jad.2019.11.036
- **PMID**: 31744739

## Affiliation
Department of Epidemiology and Biostatistics, School of Public Health, Jilin University, Changchun, China

## Abstract Summary
This study examined temporal trends in depression prevalence among U.S. adults using six NHANES cycles from 2005-2006 through 2015-2016 (N = 29,303 adults aged 20+). Depression was measured using PHQ-9, with a standard cutoff of >= 10. Weighted prevalence was estimated for each cycle and tested for linear trend. Results showed that overall depression prevalence remained relatively stable (approximately 8-9%), but severity-specific analyses revealed that severe depression (PHQ-9 >= 20) significantly increased over the study period (P-trend = 0.026). Age-stratified analyses showed that severe depression increased most notably among adults aged 65+ (P-trend < 0.001), and moderate depression increased among young adults 20-39 (P-trend = 0.028). Risk factors analysis identified lower income, current smoking, hypertension, and diabetes as major correlates of depression across all cycles. The authors concluded that while overall depression prevalence appeared stable, the increasing burden of severe depression — particularly among older adults — warrants enhanced screening and intervention.

## Key Methods
- Survey-weighted prevalence estimation per NHANES cycle
- Weighted univariate logistic regression with p-for-trend (cycle as ordinal predictor)
- Severity-stratified analysis (mild, moderate, moderately severe, severe)
- Age-stratified trend analysis (20-39, 40-64, 65+)
- Subgroup analyses by sex, race/ethnicity, income, education
- Risk factor analysis using multivariable logistic regression
- Complex survey design with cycle-specific 2-year MEC weights

## Key Findings
1. Overall depression (PHQ-9 >= 10) prevalence approximately 8-9%, appearing stable across 2005-2016
2. Severe depression (PHQ-9 >= 20) significantly increased (P-trend = 0.026)
3. Among adults 65+: severe depression increased significantly (P-trend < 0.001)
4. Among adults 20-39: moderate depression increased (P-trend = 0.028)
5. Female prevalence approximately 1.5× higher than male across all cycles
6. Major risk factors: low income (<1.0 PIR), current smoking, hypertension, diabetes
7. Non-Hispanic Black adults and those with lower education had higher depression burden

## Dataset Used
NHANES 2005-2006, 2007-2008, 2009-2010, 2011-2012, 2013-2014, 2015-2016 (6 cycles)
