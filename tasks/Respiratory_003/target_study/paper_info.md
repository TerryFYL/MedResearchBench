## Abstract Summary
This cross-sectional study investigated the association between the Metabolic Score for Insulin Resistance (METS-IR), a novel non-insulin-dependent insulin resistance index, and obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) using data from the National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) spanning 2005-2008 and 2015-2018. METS-IR was calculated as ln((2 × fasting plasma glucose) + triglycerides) × BMI / ln(HDL-cholesterol). OSA status was determined through sleep disorder questionnaires. The study explored the potential relationship between METS-IR and other insulin resistance indices (TyG, TG/HDL-C, HOMA-IR) with OSA through three multivariable logistic regression models and restricted cubic spline (RCS) curves. Subgroup analyses were conducted by age, sex, race, BMI, hypertension, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease status. Results demonstrated a significant positive association between METS-IR and OSA risk, with a nonlinear dose-response pattern. The association was notably stronger in the non-diabetic population compared to the diabetic population, suggesting that metabolic system impairment in diabetic patients may shift the primary OSA drivers to obesity, age, and genetic susceptibility.

## Key Methods
- Cross-sectional analysis of NHANES 2005-2008 and 2015-2018 (4 cycles)
- METS-IR formula: ln((2 × FPG) + TG) × BMI / ln(HDL-C)
- Comparison with TyG index, TG/HDL-C ratio, and HOMA-IR
- Survey-weighted multivariable logistic regression (3 progressive adjustment models)
- Restricted cubic splines (RCS) for dose-response visualization
- Subgroup analyses by sex, age, race, BMI, diabetes, hypertension, CVD
- Interaction testing between METS-IR and stratification variables
- Complex survey design with NHANES sampling weights

## Key Findings
1. METS-IR is significantly positively associated with OSA risk across all adjustment models
2. Nonlinear dose-response relationship between METS-IR and OSA (RCS analysis)
3. METS-IR-OSA association is significantly stronger in non-diabetic vs. diabetic populations
4. TyG, TG/HDL-C, and HOMA-IR associations with OSA were only significant in non-diabetic populations
5. Significant interaction between METS-IR and diabetes status for OSA risk
6. Sex-stratified subgroup analysis shows differential METS-IR-OSA associations between men and women
7. METS-IR offers advantage over HOMA-IR as it does not require fasting insulin measurement

## Dataset Used
NHANES 2005-2006, 2007-2008, 2015-2016, 2017-2018 cycles (fasting subsample for laboratory-based indices)
