## Abstract Summary
This study evaluated the associations of dietary sodium and potassium intake with hypertension among U.S. adults using NHANES 2017-2018 data (n=5,569 adults aged >= 20 years). Dietary intake was assessed using the Day 1 24-hour dietary recall. Multivariable logistic regression with progressive covariate adjustment and restricted cubic spline (RCS) models with 4 knots were used to examine dose-response relationships. Subgroup analyses were stratified by sex, age, and BMI. No significant associations were found between sodium or potassium intake and hypertension after full covariate adjustment (OR ~ 1.00, P > 0.05). RCS analysis showed no significant nonlinear trends. Results remained stable across sensitivity models. The authors concluded that within typical dietary intake ranges in the U.S. population, sodium and potassium may lack independent predictive utility for hypertension after adjustment for key lifestyle and demographic confounders.

## Key Methods
- Survey-weighted multivariable logistic regression (4 progressive models)
- Restricted cubic spline (RCS) with 4 knots at 5th, 35th, 65th, 95th percentiles
- Subgroup analyses by sex, age group, BMI category
- Dietary intake standardized as z-scores
- Complex survey design accounted for (weights, PSU, strata)

## Key Findings
1. No significant association between dietary sodium intake and hypertension (adjusted OR ~ 1.00)
2. No significant association between potassium intake and hypertension
3. No significant association for sodium-to-potassium ratio
4. RCS dose-response curves showed flat relationships with wide confidence intervals
5. Subgroup analyses: slightly stronger trends in older adults and obese individuals, but interaction terms not significant (all P-interaction > 0.05)
6. Sensitivity analyses confirmed robustness of null findings
