## Abstract Summary
This cross-sectional study used NHANES data from 1999 to 2016 to assess trends in self-reported hepatitis B vaccination coverage and serologic evidence of immunity among US-born children and adolescents aged 2-18 years (n=21,873). While vaccination coverage increased significantly from 62.6% (1999) to 86.3% (2016) (P < .001), a paradoxical decline in serologic evidence of vaccine-induced immunity was observed: from 64.6% to 46.5% in ages 6-10, from 68.8% to 26.2% in ages 11-13, and from 68.5% to 15.6% in ages 14-18. The 1994-2003 birth cohort demonstrated significantly reduced immunity levels. The findings suggest a possible need for surveillance and booster vaccination for young adults born after 1994 as vaccine-induced antibody levels wane over time.

## Key Methods
- Survey-weighted prevalence estimation across 9 NHANES cycles (1999-2016)
- Temporal trend analysis with logistic regression (P-for-trend)
- Age-stratified analysis (2-5, 6-10, 11-13, 14-18 years)
- Birth cohort analysis (pre-1994 vs. post-1994 universal vaccination cohorts)
- Serologic classification: vaccine-induced immunity (anti-HBs >= 10 mIU/mL with negative anti-HBc)
- Complex survey design accounted for (weights, PSU, strata)

## Key Findings
1. Vaccination coverage increased from 62.6% (95% CI: 58.6-66.4%) in 1999 to 86.3% (95% CI: 82.9-89.2%) in 2016 (P < .001)
2. Serologic immunity decreased dramatically in older children/adolescents:
   - Ages 6-10: 64.6% to 46.5%
   - Ages 11-13: 68.8% to 26.2%
   - Ages 14-18: 68.5% to 15.6%
3. Youngest children (2-5 years) maintained high immunity (~60%), consistent with recent vaccination
4. 1994-2003 birth cohort showed significantly lower immunity than earlier cohorts
5. The divergence between rising vaccination coverage and declining serologic immunity suggests waning vaccine-induced antibody levels with time since vaccination

## Dataset Used
NHANES 1999-2000 through 2015-2016 (9 consecutive 2-year cycles)
